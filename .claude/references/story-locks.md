# 6 Story Locks — Scriptwriting Reference

Source: Kallaway (1M+ followers, billions of views). These six word-level storytelling techniques lock viewer attention throughout an entire video — not just the hook. They are complementary to the Kallaway Formula (Context Lean-In → Stun Gun → Contrarian Snapback) which handles the first 4–10 seconds.

## Overview: The Attention Hourglass

Every viewer's attention runs on a timer — like an hourglass. The hook flips it at the start, buying you the first chunk of watch time. But without re-engagement, the sand runs out and viewers leave. This is the decaying retention curve most videos show after the hook.

**Story Locks solve mid-video retention.** They are word-level substitutions and phrasing patterns that reset the attention clock, close "exit doors" in the viewer's mind, and create micro-curiosity loops that keep people watching.

**Key insight**: All six locks are powered by **contrast** — the gap between what the viewer expects and what you actually say. Contrast is the foundational layer.

## 1. Term Branding (The Labeling Effect)

**Definition**: Give your concept a name. Don't just explain an idea — brand it with a memorable label.

**Psychology**: The **labeling effect** — the moment something has a name, the brain treats it as more important. The viewer thinks: *"Wait, what is that? I need to understand it."* That anticipation acts like mental quicksand — they can't leave until the term is explained.

**Before → After**:
| Weak (unnamed) | Strong (term-branded) |
|---|---|
| "Here's how to structure your AI prompts" | "This is the **Prompt Scaffolding** technique" |
| "You should test multiple agent configurations" | "I call this the **Agent Gauntlet** approach" |
| "Break your code into smaller, testable units" | "Apply the **Micro-Contract Pattern**" |

**Practical Tactic**: Before publishing, look at your 1–2 core ideas and ask: *"Could I package this and give it a name?"* The name should be short (2–3 words), vivid, and immediately intriguing.

**Placement**: Usually Scene 2–3 (early enough to create anticipation, late enough that the concept has been introduced).

**Frequency**: 1–2 coined terms per video max. Don't term-brand everything — it loses impact.

**Anti-pattern**: Naming trivial concepts. Only brand your contrarian ideas and best frameworks.

## 2. Embedded Truths (Certainty Framing)

**Definition**: Frame statements as already-true facts instead of tentative possibilities. Remove hedging language.

**Psychology**: Weak frames ("if," "maybe," "might") create **micro-moments of doubt** — tiny exit doors the viewer's attention can walk through. Certainty framing closes those doors. The viewer's brain never gets a chance to evaluate — it just follows along.

**Before → After**:
| Hedging (exit doors open) | Embedded Truth (exit doors closed) |
|---|---|
| "If you try this approach..." | "**When** you try this approach..." |
| "This might work because..." | "**The reason** this works so well..." |
| "You might notice this pattern..." | "**Once you see** this pattern, you can't unsee it" |
| "Some developers might be making this mistake..." | "**The mistake** most developers make is..." |
| "This could improve your workflow..." | "**This is how** you improve your workflow" |

**Practical Tactic**: Go through your script and search-and-replace hedging words:
- `if` → `when`
- `might/maybe/could/probably` → `the reason why` / `once you` / `this is how`
- Any phrase that sounds non-committal → state it as truth

**Placement**: Throughout the entire script. This is not scene-specific — it's a global filter.

**Frequency**: Replace ALL hedging words. This is a 100% application rule, not a "sprinkle in" technique.

**Anti-pattern**: Using embedded truths for claims you can't back up. The technique is about *framing*, not lying. Your underlying content should be accurate.

## 3. Thought Narration (The Mind-Reading Effect)

**Definition**: Say out loud what the viewer is already thinking. When done right, it feels like you're reading their mind.

**Psychology**: Two benefits: (1) **Instant trust** — if you say what's in their head, they believe you understand the problem. (2) **Retention lock** — they need to keep watching to hear your answer to the thing they were just thinking.

It turns the viewer's internal monologue into a simulated one-on-one conversation. Personalization at scale.

**Example Phrases**:
- "Now, you're probably thinking: *'That sounds great, but how does it scale?'*"
- "The question on your mind right now is: *'Does this work with existing codebases?'*"
- "If I were you, the thing that would worry me most is..."
- "You might be sitting there thinking: *'That can't possibly be faster than...'*"

**Tech/AI Niche Examples**:
| Moment | Thought Narration |
|---|---|
| After showing AI code generation | "You're thinking: *'Sure, but does it handle edge cases?'*" |
| After claiming a tool is better | "The question on your mind: *'What about the cost?'*" |
| After a bold performance claim | "You're probably wondering: *'Is this just cherry-picked benchmarks?'*" |

**Practical Tactic**: After every major point or hot take, pause and ask yourself: *"What should the viewer be thinking right now?"* Then state it at a transitional moment.

**Placement**: After major reveals or hot takes. Best at transition points between sections — usually 1–2 times per video.

**Frequency**: 1–2 instances per video. Overuse makes it feel formulaic.

**Anti-pattern**: Narrating a thought the viewer obviously isn't having. It must genuinely reflect their likely internal reaction.

## 4. Negative Frames (Loss Aversion)

**Definition**: Frame advice as a warning or mistake to avoid, rather than a positive recommendation.

**Psychology**: **Loss aversion** — research shows people are 2x more motivated to avoid pain than to seek reward. A negative frame ("Stop doing this") triggers urgency that a positive frame ("Try doing this") doesn't. The viewer thinks: *"Wait, am I doing that wrong? I need to find out."*

**Before → After (The Negative Flip)**:
| Positive Frame (weaker) | Negative Frame (stronger) |
|---|---|
| "Use this hook format" | "**Stop** making your hooks like this" |
| "Here's how to grow your AI agent business" | "This is **killing** your AI agent business" |
| "Try using more specific prompts" | "This **prompting mistake** is costing you tokens" |
| "Structure your codebase this way" | "**Don't** ship code until you fix this pattern" |
| "Here's a great testing strategy" | "The **worst** thing you could do is skip this test" |

**Practical Tactic**: Take your key points and try **inverting** them (the "Negative Flip"). Same information, but framed as what to avoid. Not every point should be negative — mix in positive frames to avoid sounding alarmist.

**Placement**: Works best in hooks (Scene 1) and at key pivot points where you need to regain attention.

**Frequency**: At least 1 negative-framed point per video. Don't overdo — if everything is a warning, nothing feels urgent.

**Anti-pattern**: Making every single point negative. A constant stream of warnings becomes exhausting and loses its punch.

## 5. Loop Openers (Attention Clock Reset)

**Definition**: A transitional phrase that confirms the value of what you just covered, then teases that the next part is even more valuable. It resets the viewer's attention hourglass.

**Psychology**: **Rehooking** — the best creators rehook every 20–30 seconds for Shorts and every 60–90 seconds for long-form. A loop opener reopens a curiosity loop just as the previous one closes, bridging the viewer from section A to section B.

**Example Phrases**:
- "Here's the thing, though..."
- "But that's not even the most interesting part."
- "And this is where it gets crazy."
- "Most people stop here — but actually..."
- "Here's what nobody talks about."
- "Okay, so that was powerful. But the next one is even more important."
- "Now, before I get to that, you need to understand this first."

**Key Detail**: The best loop openers contain a **contrast word** (but, though, actually, however). This is not coincidence — the contrast is what redirects attention from A to B.

**Practical Tactic**: After every major section, add a loop opener that transitions the viewer. Think of it as a bridge — the viewer needs to walk across to the next piece before they get bored.

**Placement**: Between major sections. If your video has 5 sections, aim for 3–4 loop openers.

**Frequency**:
- **Long-form (60s+)**: Every 60–90 seconds
- **Shorts (< 60s)**: Every 20–30 seconds
- **Very short (15s)**: Usually not applicable (script is too short)

**Anti-pattern**: Loop openers that don't deliver — if you tease "the most important part" and then show something unremarkable, you break trust.

## 6. Contrast Words (The Curiosity Gap Engine)

**Definition**: Words that create an A→B pivot, splitting a sentence into what the viewer expects and what you actually believe. The gap between A and B is the curiosity engine.

**Psychology**: Curiosity comes from a gap between what someone expects and what you believe. The bigger the gap, the more curiosity. Contrast words are the bridge that creates that gap. They take whatever the viewer thought, flip it, and force re-engagement.

**The Contrast Word Toolkit**:
| Word | Function | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **but** | Strongest pivot — creates direct A vs B comparison | "Everyone uses RAG — **but** there's a better approach" |
| **actually** | Implies what you thought was wrong | "**Actually**, the data shows the opposite" |
| **instead** | Redirects to an alternative | "**Instead** of fine-tuning, try this" |
| **turns out** | Reveals a surprising truth | "**Turns out** the bottleneck isn't the model — it's the prompt" |
| **except** | Creates an exception to the assumed rule | "Every framework does this — **except** the ones actually scaling" |
| **yet** | Implies contradiction (slightly formal) | "Everyone knows this. **Yet** almost nobody does it" |

**Practical Tactic**: Go through your key "haymaker" sentences (the big points). For each one, try splitting it in half: lean the viewer one way (A), then use a contrast word to pivot to your actual point (B).

**Placement**: At key pivot sentences throughout the script. Especially important in hooks and section transitions.

**Frequency**: Use at every major pivot point, but vary the contrast word. Don't overuse "but" — rotate through the toolkit.

**Anti-pattern**: Using contrast words for every sentence. Some points are straightforward — not everything needs an A→B structure. If it sounds "friction-heavy," you're overusing it.

## How They Connect: Contrast as the Foundation

Contrast is the bedrock that powers all six locks:

| Story Lock | How Contrast Powers It |
|---|---|
| Term Branding | The unfamiliar name **contrasts** with what the viewer expected to hear |
| Embedded Truths | Speaking with certainty **contrasts** with the uncertainty they're used to |
| Thought Narration | Hearing their own thoughts out loud **contrasts** with what they expected |
| Negative Frames | A warning **contrasts** with the advice they expected |
| Loop Openers | Teasing something new **contrasts** with what they just heard |
| Contrast Words | Directly uses A→B contrast structure |

**Takeaway**: If you only learn one thing, learn contrast. Master the A→B pivot and the other five locks become natural extensions.
