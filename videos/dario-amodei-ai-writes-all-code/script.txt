Anthropic's CEO said something in March 2025 that made every developer stop scrolling.

At the Council on Foreign Relations, Dario Amodei said this: "I think we will be there in three to six months — where AI is writing ninety percent of the code. And then in twelve months, we may be in a world where AI is writing essentially all of the code."

Not someday. Not eventually. Twelve months.

And here's what most people missed: he also said his own engineers at Anthropic already say — quote — "I don't write any code anymore. I let the model write the code, I just edit it."

So is this the end for developers? Or is it something else entirely?

Here's what he actually meant. Amodei wasn't saying engineers disappear. He was saying the thing that changes is what engineers spend their time on. The programmer still needs to specify what the conditions are, what the overall app you're building looks like, what the architecture decisions are. The code gets written. The direction still comes from you.

Think of it this way. A century ago, most writers spent hours on mechanical typewriters, retyping drafts. Today, writers still exist — they just don't spend their time on mechanical tasks. The craft shifted up a level. That's what Amodei is describing.

But the timeline was wrong. We're now past the six-month mark on that prediction. AI tools are everywhere — eighty-four percent of developers use them — but we are not in a world where AI writes ninety percent of code. The prediction was real, but the pace was off.

So what should you actually do right now?

First: stop writing code you don't need to write. Claude Code, Cursor, Copilot — use them. Engineers who use AI tools are shipping faster, not being replaced. The skill isn't coding anymore. It's orchestrating.

Second: go deeper on systems thinking. The engineers who will matter most are the ones who understand architecture, who can break a problem into pieces, evaluate what the agent built, and catch when it goes wrong. That's not a skill AI replaces. That's the skill AI amplifies.

Third: stop ignoring this. The companies not adapting now will be the ones scrambling in two years. This isn't a drill. Amodei runs one of the most powerful AI labs on earth. When he says the role of developer changes — take it seriously, even if the timeline slips.

Will AI write all the code? Probably, eventually. But the question that actually matters is: when it does, are you the person directing it — or the person it replaces?
