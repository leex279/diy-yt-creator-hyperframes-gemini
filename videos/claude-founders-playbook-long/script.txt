Chapter two of the playbook calls out what changed. The old playbook for a startup was validate, raise, hire, build, raise again, grow, hire more, repeat. Each new phase needed a bigger team, a different skill set, and a fresh funding round. Anthropic says that is over. In twenty twenty-six, A I can write production code, run market research, draft investor materials, and automate operations. Founders who never wrote a line of code are shipping production applications today. The lean ten-person unicorn used to be an underdog story. The playbook calls it a deliberate plan of action.

Chapter three. Idea stage. The goal is simple. Find a problem worth paying to solve. The playbook says every founder starts the same way. A problem they can't stop thinking about. But Anthropic flags the number one failure mode of twenty twenty-six right at the top of this chapter. Mistaking building for validating. When technical blockers disappear, an impassioned founder ships a prototype in four hours and calls it proof. It is not proof. C B Insights data the playbook cites says forty-two percent of startups still fail because they built something nobody wanted. And with agentic coding, that number is only going to climb. So what does the playbook recommend instead? Use Claude as your devil's advocate. Open a Claude chat and ask it to challenge your idea before you write a single line of code. Define the precise customer profile first. Stress-test your assumptions. Then, and only then, talk to real users. Idea stage exits when the problem is real, you know exactly who has it, and you have a concept that beats every existing alternative.

Chapter four. M V P stage. The goal is proving people will actually pay for the thing you built. And the playbook calls out a second silent killer. Falling for false product-market fit. Agentic coding makes it easy to ship fast and rack up signups that look like traction. The playbook says do not trust signup numbers. Build your measurement framework before launch. Use the Sean Ellis test. Ask active users how disappointed they would be if your product disappeared tomorrow. Forty percent answering very disappointed is the real product-market fit gate. The playbook also names a second M V P killer specific to A I-native startups. Agentic technical debt. Skip the architectural specs, skip the claude dot M D file, and every Claude Code session re-derives your decisions from scratch. The codebase drifts further from any plan you had. The playbook says five minutes of documentation per session is cheap insurance against a codebase that eventually collapses. M V P stage exits when you have product-market fit, a measurement framework that works, and a codebase that won't betray you the moment you scale.

Chapter five. Launch stage. The job changes. You have product-market fit. Now you are going from one founder to a small team, from a handful of users to real paying customers. The playbook flags the failure mode that ends most startups at this exact moment. The founder becomes the bottleneck. At M V P stage, the founder being in every decision loop was an asset. At Launch stage, that same pattern means a one-hour decision now takes a week. Hiring stalls. Customer escalations sit in your inbox. The playbook says the fix is operational. Build your first systems. Move ops work out of your head into Claude Cowork for the non-code workflows. Move shipping work into Claude Code with proper specs and review loops. Anthropic gives a concrete sub-pattern. Feed Claude Code's audit findings back to Claude itself for triage and sequencing. Decide what gets fixed by who, in what order. Launch stage exits when you have repeatable customer acquisition, around ten paying customers, and decisions that no longer go through you for every line of code.

Chapter six. Scale stage. The founder's role recenters one more time. From builder to publisher. Your Launch-stage systems graduate into enterprise-grade infrastructure. Claude moves from your personal tool to a company-wide capability. The playbook says the Scale-stage challenge is the inverse of Launch. Now you have to actually trust the systems you built. Delegating before they exist is a fast way back to chaos. But Anthropic also points at the long-term moat. Workflow lock-in. Compounding data network effects. Integrations so deep that switching cost outpaces feature parity. The playbook says use Claude across the whole company to build that moat. Product marketing translates your value props at scale. Sales operations get repeatable playbooks. Customer success runs on context Claude already has. Scale stage exits when the company is sustainable, even when the founder is no longer directly in the work.
