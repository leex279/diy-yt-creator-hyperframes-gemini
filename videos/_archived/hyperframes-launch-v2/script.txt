A I agents can write. They can talk. They can code. They operate autonomously. But one thing has stayed just out of reach. Editing video. HeyGen open-sourced HyperFrames to fix that. And here's the kicker. This entire video was made by Claude Code. Using HyperFrames itself.

Every video tool today has the same flaw. After Effects. DaVinci Resolve. Premiere. They are all built on JSON or X M L timelines. Layers. Keyframes. Years of human muscle memory. None of that lives in agent training data. So agents stumble. Every time. The toolchain was the bottleneck.

Now look at what agents do know. They were trained on the entire web. Billions of H T M L pages. Millions of C S S and JavaScript animations. Hundreds of thousands of G S A P snippets, S V G compositions, and canvas experiments. The web is the largest creative medium in their training data, by orders of magnitude. H T M L is their native tongue.

So HyperFrames takes their best work, and adds a thin layer. Three data attributes. data start. data duration. data track index. That's it. Add them to any H T M L element and you have a video timeline. The agent writes regular H T M L, C S S, and JavaScript. HyperFrames turns it into M P 4. Or M O V. Or web M.

And here's the part that breaks people's brains. Anything that works in a browser, works in HyperFrames. C S S animations. G S A P timelines. Lottie. Three dot J S. D 3 visualizations. Google Fonts. Whatever the agent already knows from the open web, it can use directly. No wrappers. No framework to learn.

For a long time, the models weren't quite there. Code generation worked, but quality wasn't reliable. Then November 2025 happened. Gemini 3 and Opus 4 point 5 previewed. HeyGen swapped them in. And the agent started producing motion graphics and multi-scene B rolls. Consistently. With high quality. That was the moment HyperFrames converged.
