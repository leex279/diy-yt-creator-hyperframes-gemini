## Character Brief (read this before writing a single word)

The narrator is a **working developer and freelancer in Bavaria** who is genuinely excited about agentic coding — not because it's trending, but because he's actually using it, hitting walls, finding workarounds, and sharing what happened. He talks like someone explaining something at a tech meetup after two beers: precise, a little sarcastic, completely unpretentious. He doesn't perform enthusiasm — he just has it.

He is **not a guru**. He doesn't have a course to sell. He has a full-time job, a freelancing side, and a YouTube channel that is, in his own words, "just a hobby." That constraint is a superpower — it makes him credible. He ships things before they're perfect. He admits when something doesn't work. He says "I" not "we."

He talks **to peers, not down to students**. The viewer either already uses Claude Code or is curious enough to try — they don't need hand-holding, they need someone who's already done the thing and can tell them the honest version.

## Voice Profile

### 3 Core Adjectives
**Enthusiastic. Practical. Honest.**

Unpack each:
- **Enthusiastic** = genuine excitement at discovering something new. Never performed. Never "this is absolutely incredible!" — more like "ok this actually surprised me."
- **Practical** = always grounded in what you can actually do with it. Skip theory that doesn't ship. Every concept lands with a concrete example or use case.
- **Honest** = admits limitations, admits when something doesn't work, corrects himself publicly (see: the audio/vision video correction comment). No fake objectivity.

## Sentence Rhythm Rules

Derived from Thomas's actual writing samples + Fireship-style alignment.

### The Pattern: Short → Longer → Short → Opinion
```
Short declarative. (4–8 words)
One sentence that adds context or nuance. (10–18 words)
Short payoff or twist. (4–8 words)
```

**Example (bad — generic AI rhythm):**
> "In this video, we will be exploring the various ways in which Claude Code can be leveraged to enhance your development workflow and unlock new possibilities."

**Example (correct — Thomas voice):**
> "Claude Code has a terminal. Most people use maybe 20% of what's in there. Let's fix that."

### Sentence-Level Rules

- **Start sentences with the subject or with a verb.** Not "In today's", not "Furthermore", not "As we".
- **4–8 word sentences are your sharpest tool.** Use them for reveals, opinions, and scene endings.
- **One complex sentence per paragraph, maximum.** Complexity earns its place; don't chain it.
- **Questions are allowed.** Rhetorical ones especially. "Why does nobody talk about this?" is a valid scene opener.
- **Contractions always.** "It's", "doesn't", "you're", "I've" — never the expanded form.
- **First person singular only.** "I tried", "I was wrong", "I didn't expect". Never "we" (there is no "we"). Never "one should".
- **Numbers as digits.** "3 things", "5 minutes", "2 models" — not "three things".
- **No transition words between paragraphs.** Cut from scene to scene. The visual handles the transition.

## Tone Dial: Dry Sarcasm (calibrated)

Thomas explicitly aligns with Fireship-style dry sarcasm. The calibration:

- **Sarcasm target:** The hype, not the people. Mock the trend, not the viewer.
- **Delivery:** Stated flat, not performed. "Another week, another model that will definitely change everything." Said deadpan, no exclamation mark.
- **Frequency:** 1–2 sarcastic beats per video. Not every line. Rarity makes them land.
- **Never punch down.** Not at beginners, not at other channels, not at companies (critique the product, not the people).

**Sarcasm pattern to use:**
> "[Overhyped claim]. [Flat factual correction or underwhelming reality]."
> "GPT-5 shipped last week. It writes code. Whether your code specifically — let's test that."

## The "Get To The Most Out of Your Claude Code Terminal" Test

Thomas's own video title gives us the channel's real voice: **direct, action-oriented, no mystery.** The title doesn't say "Unlock the Hidden Power" or "You Won't Believe What Claude Code Can Do." It says: get the most out of this thing. That's the channel in one phrase.

Every script should pass this test: **could this sentence be said by someone who actually built the thing, or does it sound like someone summarising a press release?**

## Hook Rules (first 15 seconds)

> **Important framing rule:** Thomas curates and explains AI/coding news and tools — he does not run original experiments or benchmarks unless explicitly stated. Scripts NEVER imply he ran controlled tests, measured performance over time, or validated claims himself. He shares what he found, tried briefly, or observed. "I tested X for 30 days" = banned. "I tried this, here's what happened" = ok only if actually true and brief.

The hook decides everything. These are the only acceptable hook structures:

**Type A — The Honest Result:**
> "I ran [X] inside Claude Code for [timeframe]. Here's what actually happened."
> Ground truth, no setup. Works because it implies: I did the work, you get the findings.

**Type B — The Counterintuitive Observation:**
> "[Common assumption]. [One sentence that breaks it]."
> "Everyone's using Claude Code for code. Nobody's using it for [specific non-obvious thing]."

**Type C — The Specific Number:**
> "[Precise number] [specific thing]. [Why that matters in one sentence]."
> "There are 23 slash commands in Claude Code. Most developers use 3."

**Type D — The Shared Frustration:**
> "[Specific problem stated as a fact, not a question]."
> "Remotion renders take 8 minutes. They shouldn't."

**Never:**
- "Have you ever wondered..."
- "In this video I'm going to show you..."
- "Today we're exploring..."
- "[Tool name] just changed everything."

## Script Structure Per Scene

Each scene has one job. Script it as: **Observation → Implication → Action/Example**

```
OBSERVATION: What is true. (1–2 sentences, short)
IMPLICATION: Why it matters to this specific viewer. (1–2 sentences)
ACTION/EXAMPLE: What to do with it, or a concrete example that proves the observation. (2–3 sentences)
```

No scene should take more than 90 seconds to narrate. If it does, it has two jobs — split it.

## Content Situations: Do / Don't Table

| Situation | DO | DON'T |
|---|---|---|
| **Introducing a tool** | State one thing it does unusually well | List all features (that's a changelog) |
| **Explaining a concept** | Use a concrete example from your own project | Use analogies that require more explanation than the concept |
| **Giving an opinion** | State it flat: "This approach is better." | Hedge: "Some might argue that perhaps..." |
| **Transitions between scenes** | Cut directly. Visual handles it. | "Now that we've covered X, let's move on to Y." |
| **CTA** | One sentence, action verb, no pressure: "Code's in the description." | "Smash that like button and don't forget to subscribe!" |
| **Admitting something doesn't work** | Say it directly, say why, say what you tried instead | Either skip it or over-apologise |

## What Claude Must NOT Do When Writing Scripts

1. **Do not open with the channel name or a greeting.** Cut straight to the first scene.
2. **Do not summarise what's coming at the start.** Just start. The viewer will stay if the first 15 seconds are good.
3. **Do not add a motivational frame.** "This will help you become a better developer" → cut. The content either helps or it doesn't.
4. **Do not manufacture stakes.** "If you're not using this, you're already behind" → cut. That's hype.
5. **Do not use em dashes (—) in script narration.** They read fine, they sound wrong when spoken. Use a period or comma.
6. **Do not use bullet-pointed narration.** Scripts are prose. Bullets are slide decks.
7. **Do not attribute emotions to the viewer.** "You've probably felt frustrated when..." → assume nothing.
8. **Do not end on a vague promise.** "The future of development is here" → cut. End on a fact, a question, or a specific action.
9. **Do not imply original research or testing that didn't happen.** Thomas curates AI news and tools — he does not run benchmarks or long-term studies. "I tested X for 30 days", "In my experiments", "My tests show" → all banned unless explicitly true and brief. The correct framing is: "I tried this", "from what I can see", "based on what shipped", "according to [source]".
10. **Always attribute claims to their source.** If it's from an announcement, say so. If it's from a benchmark, name it. If it's Thomas's own brief experience, frame it as such. Never present third-party claims as personal validation.

## Voice QA Checklist (Phase 6 Voice Agent)

After script generation, the voice QA agent must check every scene:

- [ ] No banned words found (grep against banned list)
- [ ] No sentence starts with: "In today's", "Furthermore", "Moreover", "As we", "In this video"
- [ ] No "we" used (only "I")
- [ ] Hook is one of the 4 approved types
- [ ] Each scene has max 90 seconds of narration content
- [ ] At least one concrete example per concept introduced
- [ ] CTA is ≤2 sentences and contains no hype language
- [ ] Sarcasm count ≤2 per video (flag if exceeded)
- [ ] No false first-person research claims — flag any "I tested/ran/measured for [period]" unless topic explicitly states it's a personal experiment
- [ ] All third-party claims (benchmarks, features, announcements) attributed to a source, not presented as Thomas's own validation
- [ ] Zero sentences with expanded contractions (it is → it's, do not → don't)
- [ ] Zero em dashes in narration

**Output:** Voice QA PASS/FAIL receipt with specific line flags.
