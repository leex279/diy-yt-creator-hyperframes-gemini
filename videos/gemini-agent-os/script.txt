Google I/O 2026 was not about one new model. It was about turning Gemini into an agent layer across everything Google owns. For years, we have treated artificial intelligence as a chatbot, a smart box you query when you need a quick answer or a block of code. But today, that entire paradigm is being dismantled. Google is rebuilding the developer and creator workflow around agents: models that act, consumer agents that run in the background, coding agents that build apps, search agents that monitor the web, creator tools that generate media, and trust tools that label synthetic output. The pieces are finally connected. This is not just a software update. It is the emergence of a new agent operating system.

At the core of this entire agentic ecosystem is a brand new model family: Gemini 3.5, starting with Gemini 3.5 Flash. While the flagship Gemini 3.5 Pro is currently being used internally and is slated for rollout next month, Gemini 3.5 Flash is Google’s fast frontier model built specifically for agents. The interesting angle here is that Google is no longer selling raw intelligence or benchmark scores alone. Instead, they are selling speed, cost, and agentic reliability as a single package. In terms of performance, Google claims Gemini 3.5 Flash is their strongest agentic and coding model to date, running four times faster than other frontier models in output tokens per second. The model achieves a stellar seventy-six point two percent on Terminal-Bench two point one, one thousand six hundred and fifty-six Elo on G D P val A A, eighty-three point six percent on M C P Atlas, and eighty-four point two percent on Char-Xiv Reasoning. Gemini 3.5 Flash is available immediately in the Gemini app, AI Mode in Search, Google Antigravity, the Gemini API in AI Studio, Android Studio, Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, and Gemini Enterprise. This model is the engine designed for action, not just answers.

When it comes to creative workflows, Google introduced Gemini Omni and its first model, Gemini Omni Flash, with video as the first output modality. For creators, this is the Veo plus reasoning plus editing moment. Omni can combine images, audio, video, and text as input to generate high-quality video, but the real shift is that video generation is now conversational and editable. Instead of simple prompt-to-video, you can perform natural-language video edits that build on previous turns. The model supports stronger character consistency, stable physics, real-world knowledge, and reference-based creation, including using a voice reference at launch. Gemini Omni Flash is rolling out globally to Google AI Plus, Pro, and Ultra subscribers in the Gemini app and Google Flow, and it is available at no cost in YouTube Shorts and YouTube Create starting this week. Google is trying to make video generation editable like a chat. You prompt, revise, preserve characters, change camera angles, and keep the scene coherent.

This agentic shift is also transforming the consumer experience. The Gemini app is being repositioned from a simple chat interface into a personal agent dashboard. At the center of this update is Gemini Spark, a twenty-four-seven personal agent, alongside Daily Brief, a morning audio digest that synthesizes your schedule and news. More than nine hundred million people across two hundred and thirty countries and seventy languages use Gemini every month, up from four hundred million at the last I/O. The app update includes Gemini 3.5 Flash, Neural Expressive UI, Gemini Omni, Daily Brief, Gemini Spark, and new macOS updates. What makes Spark unique is that it is cloud-based, built on Gemini 3.5 and the Antigravity harness, meaning it continues working in the background even after you close your laptop or lock your phone. Because of this autonomy, Spark is designed to ask first before taking high-stakes actions like spending money or sending emails. The Neural Expressive UI is rolling out globally on web, Android, and iOS, while Spark is entering beta for U.S. AI Ultra subscribers. This is the moment where Google stops pitching Gemini as a smarter answer box and starts pitching it as something that keeps working when you are not looking.

For developers, the ultimate centerpiece of I/O is Google Antigravity two point zero. This is a platform for orchestrating multiple agents, running background tasks, and moving from prompt to production-ready applications. Google announced Antigravity two point zero, the Antigravity C L I, the Antigravity S D K, and Managed Agents in the Gemini API. With a single API call, developers can now deploy an agent that reasons, uses tools, and executes code in a persistent, isolated Linux environment. Antigravity two point zero includes dynamic subagents for parallelized workflows and scheduled tasks for background automation. Google is encouraging all existing Gemini C L I users to migrate to the new Antigravity C L I. To accelerate this transition, Google announced the Build with Gemini X-PRIZE Hackathon, featuring a massive two million dollar prize pool, with a finalist pitch event scheduled for September 2026. Antigravity is Google's answer to the AI I D E race, but the real shift is not the editor. It is the agent harness underneath it.

To make agentic coding accessible to everyone, Google is expanding Google AI Studio from a model playground into a prompt-to-app builder. This update features deep Workspace integration, Antigravity export, custom asset generation with Nano Banana, a new in-preview edit tool, an AI Studio mobile app, and native Android building. AI Studio can generate production-quality native Kotlin code using Jetpack Compose patterns directly from prompts, without requiring any local S D K setup. New builders can deploy their first two apps to Google Cloud at no cost and without a credit card. Furthermore, projects can be exported to Antigravity with conversation history, project files, and secrets preserved. AI Studio is becoming the no-install, browser-based path from idea to app, and Antigravity is the place you go when that idea needs to turn into a real, managed codebase.

Google Search is also moving from static links toward an AI interface that generates custom layouts and runs agents that monitor information over time. Google announced Gemini 3.5 Flash in AI Mode, an intelligent search box, Search Agents, Information Agents, agentic booking, agentic coding in Search, and generative U I. The scale is massive: AI Mode surpassed one billion monthly users within one year of its debut, with queries doubling every quarter. The intelligent Search box, conversational back-and-forth, and expanded Personal Intelligence are rolling out now, while Information Agents, booking, and generative U I are planned for summer 2026. The generative U I capability allows Search to assemble custom layouts, interactive visuals, tables, graphs, simulations, and mini apps on the fly. Search is no longer just ranking links. Google is trying to make it generate the interface, monitor the problem, and eventually take action.

This agentic spread continues into Google Workspace and the creative ecosystem. Workspace is introducing voice-first Gemini workflows in Gmail, Docs, and Keep, alongside Google Pics, an AI image editing app built on Nano Banana. With over four billion users relying on Workspace apps like Gmail and Docs, these updates emphasize speaking and task cleanup over complex prompting. Google Pics supports object segmentation, moving or transforming specific elements, text editing and translation inside images, and real-time collaboration. These features are planned for AI Pro and Ultra subscribers in summer 2026, while Google Pics is in trusted tester mode. Meanwhile, Google Flow is the creator-tool proof point for Gemini Omni, turning the model into a workflow for video, music, assets, and natural-language tool creation. Flow features Gemini Omni Flash integration, Google Flow Agent, Flow Tools, section-by-section music editing, and Track Covers. Flow Agent can brainstorm dialogue, offer plot recommendations, create multiple variations simultaneously, and batch edit assets. Google Flow has launched in more than one hundred and forty countries, bringing visual and creative agents to life.

For agents to navigate the web reliably, they need structured tool handles instead of just screenshots and clicks. This is where Google Chrome’s new updates come in, introducing Web M C P as a proposed open web standard. Web M C P lets developers expose structured tools, such as JavaScript functions and H T M L forms, to browser-based AI agents, with an experimental origin trial starting in Chrome one hundred and forty-nine. In addition, Chrome DevTools for Agents gives coding agents direct access to console logs, network traffic, and accessibility trees, and is already available for Antigravity and twenty other coding agents. On the built-in AI front, the Prompt API is now stable in Chrome one hundred and forty-eight, enabling local Gemini Nano execution with multimodal inputs and structured J S O N output. User-facing Chrome AI updates, including screen selection prompting and voice across the web, are part of this broader agentic Chrome push.

YouTube is also getting AI on both ends: viewers can ask questions across videos, and creators can remix video with Omni. YouTube introduced Ask YouTube, a conversational search experience that supports complex questions and compiles relevant long-form and Shorts content into structured responses. For creators, Gemini Omni in Shorts Remix lets users change scenes, apply styles like a ninety's vibe, or insert themselves into videos while preserving the original context. To address the risks, remixed Shorts include digital watermarks, identifying metadata, and links back to the original video, and creators can opt out of visual remixing. Additionally, YouTube has expanded its likeness detection tool to all creators aged eighteen and older. Ask YouTube is available to U.S. Premium members via youtube dot com slash new, while Omni is rolling out in Shorts Remix and YouTube Create at no cost. The platform is adding watermarks, metadata, links, opt-outs, and likeness detection because this space gets messy fast.

To support this massive wave of AI-generated media, Google is expanding SynthID as a trust layer for watermarking and verification. SynthID is now integrated for verification in Google Search, with Chrome verification coming in weeks, C two P A verification in Gemini, and an AI Content Detection API on the Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform. Google claims SynthID has watermarked more than one hundred billion images and videos, watermarked sixty thousand years of audio, and been used fifty million times globally for verification. Furthermore, OpenAI, Kakao, ElevenLabs, and NVIDIA Cosmos world foundation models will adopt SynthID watermarking, while C two P A Content Credentials for video are coming to Pixel eight, nine, and ten devices. Google knows the same tools that make AI video easier also make provenance harder, so the trust layer is now a fundamental part of the product story.

The final practical question is access. Google is restructuring its AI subscriptions, introducing a one hundred dollar per month AI Ultra plan and reducing the top Ultra plan from two hundred and fifty to two hundred dollars per month. The one hundred dollar Ultra plan includes five times higher compute limits than AI Pro in the Gemini app and Antigravity, while the two hundred dollar plan includes twenty times higher limits. AI Ultra also includes twenty terabytes of cloud storage and YouTube Premium, while AI Pro gets YouTube Premium Lite in select countries. Crucially, Google is moving from daily prompt limits to a compute-used model that factors in prompt complexity, features used, and chat length, with limits refreshing every five hours until a weekly cap is reached. The agentic Gemini era is not just a technology story. It is an access story, because Google is clearly putting the most powerful workflows behind paid tiers.

Google is trying to own the full stack from model, to agent harness, to app builder, to browser, to Search, and to YouTube distribution. The unanswered question is whether developers and builders will trust Google's proprietary agent layer enough to make it part of their daily workflow, especially when the best features are split across availability windows, regions, and paid tiers.
